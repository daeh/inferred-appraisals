Social Cognitive Neuroscience Laboratory

In this experiment, we are interested in how people make judgments about
other people's emotion reactions.

This HIT consists of 8 trials in which you will read short descriptions of events and rate what emotions you think the people in those events would experience, and 6 emotion matching questions.


Note: • This HIT requires that you are fluent (expert) in English.
• You will need to be able to play audio in order to successfully complete this HIT.
• This HIT requires that you have a moderately fast internet connection (in order to load the video/audio media).

Instructions 1 of 5:

In this experiment, you will read 8 short event descriptions and then judge the emotions of a person in the event. These are followed by 6 short matching questions.


All of the people you will consider are contestants in the final round of a game show.

Over the course of the show, these two contestants have interacted in games involving strategic deception and honesty to add money to the total jackpot.

In this final round, all of the money that the two contestants have accumulated is at stake. These jackpots can range from $1.00 to over $100,000.00.


Each contestant is given a choice: whether to 'Split' or 'Steal'.

If both players choose to 'Split', they each win half of the total jackpot.

If one player chooses to 'Steal' while the other player chooses to 'Split', the player who Stole wins the entire jackpot while the player who Split wins nothing.

If both players choose to 'Steal', then they both leave having won nothing.

Instructions 2 of 5:



During each trial, you will be presented with information about the jackpot and the players' decisions, and then asked to rate the extent to which a particular player experienced 20 different emotions.

Note that if one contestant chooses 'Split', the other contestant necessarily wins
money (either half the jackpot or the entire jackpot).

If one contestant chooses 'Steal', the other contestant necessarily wins nothing
(while the first contestant wins either the entire jackpot or also leaves with nothing).

Prior to revealing their decisions to one another, the contestants have negotiated with each other in the hopes of convincing the other person to choose 'Split', often by asserting that they themselves will also 'Split'.

In order to convince the other player to 'Split', players tend to place their reputation on the line, make bold appeals to their honor and morals, and/or describe the extreme consequences that they would suffer if they were to 'Steal'. ("I promised my kids I would Split and they would never look up to me again if they saw me Steal", "I would definitely get fired from my job if I Stole", etc.)

After negotiating, the contestants simultaneously reveal their decisions by showing each other the word 'Split' or 'Steal' written inside of a gold-colored ball.

(Again, for the first player to win anything, the second player needs to 'Split', but there is no financial incentive for either player to 'Split'.)


The entire event takes place in front of a live studio audience, is broadcast on TV nationally, and is widely viewed. Contestants on the show are often recognized by strangers who remember the contestant's on-air behavior.

Instructions 5 of 5:




For each person you consider, you will be told the total size of the jackpot, the decision that each player made, and how much each player won as a result. You will also be shown a photo of the contestant that you are providing emotion ratings for.

The photo was taken in the seconds immediately leading up to the contestants revealing their decisions. The photo you are shown is NOT of the person's reaction to the decision. You should therefore not judge the person's emotions at the time of the photo, but rather judge what emotions you expect the person would have felt directly after learning the outcome of the 'Split'/'Steal' game.



Use the photo to help you think about how that particular person felt as a result of the outcome.

If you think that the person is definitely not experiencing a particular emotion, move the sliding marker all the way to the left to indicate "not any". (Whereas, if you do think that the person is experiencing the emotion, even slightly or subtly, move the marker to the right accordingly.)
